## JE #1

**[2026-06-05T15:59:15Z]**

For this journal entry, I will attempt a utopian / dystopian reading of How I Live Now, a 2013 film directed by Kevin Macdonald and available on Amazon Prime. The story follows Daisy, a young woman on holiday from the US visiting her cousins in the English countryside whose cautious exploration of love and family is harshly disrupted in the wake of a nuclear blast and subsequent military forced relocation.

While the film does not directly speak to the integration of AI or education at the heart of this course, it reflects the different readings of our module quite well. For instance, much like the Black Mirror episode "Be Right Back," the protagonist experiences the loss of her beloved and seeks desperately to reunite. The contrast here is that while in Black Mirror the artificial synthesis fails to bring back true love, in How I live Now the protagonist recovers her private utopia despite drastic, trauma-induced changes to her lover's superficial being, recognizing him as fundamentally unaltered and deserving of her commitment.

In another reflection of this week's readings of dystopia as a mirror of societal constraints on human flourishing, the remarkable personal growth of the protagonist stands out. Initially absorbed by intrusive thoughts characteristic of consumerist modernity, by the end of the narrative she directs her energy outward to survive the wilderness and care for those she loves. This shift takes place against the backdrop of the failure of the adult world as exemplified first by her aunt jetting off to international peace talks leaving her charges to fend for themselves and then wider societal collapse as the brutal realities of war unfold.

The movie also touches upon the dystopian mechanisms of control experienced by 21st-century global citizens. The protagonist is visited by an American embassy envoy, suggesting a form of tracking system that allowed them to locate her in the first place and highlighting how citizenship is leveraged to manage privileges including escaping a war zone. On the flip side, as she finds herself scavenging food scraps in a make shift labor camps the absence of integrated tracking allows for her relatively seamless escape as she makes her way back to her private utopia.

Lastly, in the context of How I live Now, Robert Frost's poem "Mending Wall" speaks to the nature of privacy and a place to call home. As the protagonist opens up from her personal isolation characterized by her private thoughts and her over the ear headphones she comes to create a carefree place shared with others. While necessarily delineated from broader society and marking a break with the natural absence of clear borders, in this case however it isn't nature's manner of slowly resisting the maintenance of artificial borders that is highlighted, but the war machine's violent disregard for the private sphere.

What I particularly enjoyed about this movie experience given the background from our assigned readings and media was observing how dystopia and utopia function at both personal and societal level as archetypes to organize human activity. The personal affirmation the protagonist receives from discovering a place that she could not have possibly imagined exists is what shapes her later conviction to reclaim what was lost. Even when the utopia is irrecoverable and her new reality might objectively be experienced as less than what it once was, the emotional blueprint it provides is what makes flourishing possible.

For this week's journal entry I want to first focus on the intensely methodical review article by Chen et al. (2022) on AI in Education (AIEd). Most of all, I appreciate the timing of the article just before the landscape shifting ChatGPT 3.5 release in 2022, representing a perfect opportunity for perceiving the field prior to the flood of discussion around GenAI in education. Concerning the 8 topics identified by the authors (Intelligent Tutoring Systems for special education, Natural Language Processing for language education, Educational robots for AI education, Educational Data Mining for performance prediction, Discourse analysis in Computer Science Collaborative Learning, Neural networks for teaching evaluation, Affective computing for learner emotion detection, Recommender systems for personalized learning), the underlying technology has either been replaced or significantly augmented by the generative and synthesis properties of large language models (LLM).

At the same time, the co-occurring characteristics of this transformative technology as inherently non-deterministic, potentially biased, and largely controlled by profit-seeking corporations represent an interesting dystopian angle just as the utopian promise of highly intelligent and adaptable technologies has become approximately fulfilled. Much like Orwell's unease with industrial automation as relayed in Claeys' (2010) nuanced account of his anti-utopian literary stance, the "combination of mass-production techniques with a techno-centric aesthetic" or 'Fordification' is not generally seen as compatible with the goals of education in a democracy. Accordingly, GPT-aware bibliometic analyses of AIEd (Rosa & Yokomizo, 2026) reflect a deep concern over the uncritical use of this new technology.

Against this background my viewing of Blade Runner and reading of the novel A Clockwork Orange, both of which engage with the cognitive and behavioral evolution of their protagonists toward transformational changes, contributed to an appreciation of the enlightenment ideals reflected in today's education such as critical thinking and reasoned character development. Yet in an age that for many brought the realization of utopias of the past, the increasing threats to democracy from "real-world authoritarians" and the externalities of an inequality breeding economic system require not just more critical reflection but also space for autonomy and alternative forms of existence, a way to escape the machine.

Claeys, G. (2010). The origins of dystopia: Wells, Huxley and Orwell. In G. Claeys (Ed.) The Cambridge companion to utopian literature (pp. 107-132). Cambridge University Press.

  For anyone else who read A Clockwork Orange and took notice of the milk bar reference in Claeys, apparently those were real things according to Claude:

  "Milk bars were a genuine and widespread institution, particularly in Britain and Australia from the 1930s onward.

  In Britain, the milk bar arrived in 1935 when the first one opened on Oxford Street in London, modelled loosely on American soda fountains. The idea was to provide a respectable, non-alcoholic alternative to the pub — cheap milk-based drinks, milkshakes, light snacks, a counter to sit at. They spread rapidly through working-class and lower-middle-class areas through the late 1930s and 1940s, and were associated with a certain modernist, American-influenced aesthetic: chrome fittings, neon, clean lines. Which is exactly what Orwell's narrator is reacting against in Coming Up For Air — the milk bar he enters is precisely this type of establishment, and his disgust is at its fakery and transatlantic pretension.

  In Australia the milk bar became an even more deeply embedded institution — essentially the corner shop, selling confectionery, newspapers, drinks, and groceries, and remaining a feature of suburban life well into the late twentieth century. The Australian milk bar has an almost nostalgic cultural status now that the British version does not quite have.

  By the time Burgess was writing A Clockwork Orange in 1962, the milk bar was a recognizable, slightly seedy fixture of British street life, associated with teenagers, late hours, and a vaguely disreputable edge — which is precisely why the Korova works as a setting. It takes something mundane and socially marginal and pushes it into dystopian exaggeration."

## JE #4

**[2026-06-28T15:38:36Z]**

This week featured an interesting juxtaposition for me as my jury service for a criminal trial came to an end just as I began diving into the original short story and later movie versions of Minority Report. Despite the embellishments, fanfare and stylistic freedoms Steven Spielberg exercises to make the movie fit the model of a Hollywood blockbuster, it is clear that the central themes from the short story carry through: what are the ethics of a perfect society, how does truth transpire in such a society, and what consequences arise from monopolizing and even automating the fact finding process underlying what is asserted as truth.

These themes find parallels in the media and readings from Doctorow and Williamson, respectively, which anticipate or document the tension between technological systems, the reality of their production and the mission and values of education in a democracy. In particular, the assertions about the role of technology in classrooms may foreclose educators' trained expertise and judgment, in effect outsourcing pedagogical decisions and freedoms to centralized systems out of their purview. Given the publication date of Williamsons introduction in 2017, none of these concerns are unique to AI but may be accelerated as cultural beliefs about AI's utility and indeed inevitability become more pervasive.

Ultimately, by performing an adjudication of the quality of students' real-time and past contributions, education also presages what students may hope to aspire to beyond the classroom. Given this responsibility, and with my jury instructions still fresh in mind, I believe it is important to continue practicing adoption with an open mind while carefully weighing the evidence. For every practice that AI undercuts, how many new ones open up, and for whom? Under which circumstances are AI systems supportive of unbiased fact finding and fair deliberations about essential truths underlying these practices?

## JE #5

**[2026-07-02T16:29:00Z]**

I'm going to try and flesh out the overlap between the non-fiction media and reading assignments this week, which focused on the nature of data and algorithmic processing in government, and H.G. Wells' The Sleeper Awakes which primarily speaks to the responsibility rulers have to their charges in light of universal human rights and equality.

As Bennett Moses discusses in her TEDx talk, rule-based processing is not a novel government instinct, but the transparency and accountability of the systems governing those rules is becoming strained in the age of AI. This is echoed by Engstrom and and Loewen whose panel contributions focus on potential mechanisms for ensuring fairness via benchmarks on the one hand and widespread skepticism toward automated decision-making in experimental conditioned scenarios. Vida Fernandez adds to these concerns additional considerations about the role of private enterprise in providing government AI systems and how in some domains transparency could undermine fairness given private actors may come to exploit automated decision frameworks.

Implied in these perspectives is that accountable automation of government is contingent on the government already adhering to standards of transparency and fairness as well as exhibiting high levels of capacity and adaptability prior to developing or commissioning these systems, including benchmarks. I believe this is where the reading of The Sleeper Awakes connects to the discussion of data-driven government. As illustrated in Graham's progression from sheer disbelief to accommodating his newfound circumstance to finally acting on his convictions, his experience suggests that a data-driven mode of governance alone is insufficient without stable anchors that account for the full picture and the history of political struggle.

As Graham discovers over time, the legal apparatus built up around his persistent sleep state performed in the rational self interest characteristic of a corporation to transform the world into an ever expanding catalog of property. Even when the system gets successfully overthrown as a consequence of Ostrog's political intrigues, it remains firmly entrenched in the apparatus of power that perpetuated widespread misery in favor of a machinistic understanding of citizens' fates. Until Graham is exposed to the condition of the working class, and despite his memories of largely pre-industrial "idyllicized" small town England, his own conscience isn't awakened to how he must act.

Ultimately, of course, The Sleeper begins to account for unfiltered reality and references it to his enlightened understanding of what it means to be the ruler. He comes to understand the nature of government of the people as a sacrifice for the greater good. Needless to say, capturing this ambition in a data stream for processing by a fixed set of rules is out of the question. And while AI adds a novel element of synthesis to the machinery of predictive ruling and rule-making, it is nothing like an objective truth engine that would make automatic government fair and transparent on its own.

## JE #6

**[2026-07-13T03:17:52Z]**

This week was difficult to process as it's hard to imagine the kind of technological developments that don't feed into the spiral of increasing impact on the environment. I appreciated the candid perspective offered by Selwyn on what technology would actually have to look like to be compatible with planetary boundaries and it's interesting to see him apply his anti-solutionism lens to the tech sector itself. Connecting to his broader thoughts on technology in education introduced previously in this course and motivated by the sociological perspective of AI governance, I began to wonder what the response to greater awareness of the technology sector's impact on the environment might look like (perhaps templated after the oil industry's misinformation campaign).

Having quite readily shed the marketing image of climate- and environmentally friendly stewardship as the political winds have shifted, I began to imagine targeted misinformation to shed technology's or more specifically AI's 'weight' problem. As science and the public uncovers more details about the resource impacts (which are difficult to ascertain but still more easily measurable than the long-term impacts on wild life and wild lands), I believe it's unlikely companies can successfully reassert the metaphor of the weightless cloud operating unobtrusively in the background. Instead they might opt to promote a sort of body positivity or anti body-shaming tactic such as proclaiming that 'AI answers are worth it' or contrasting individuals' "footprints" with those of machines made to serve the masses and indeed the future prospects of humanity. In the context of economic pressures and demands for technological innovation, companies may feel incentivized to frame their own operations as in service of long-term progress and contrast it with the relatively meaningless pursuits of everyday people when it comes to the grand ambitions of a space-bound civilization.

In fact, I explored using the expanding capabilities of today's AI [1] to generate yet another artifact for my course portfolio which I imagined could be achieved with a prompt to build a simple arcade-style game as sketched below. But, for now, I'm satisfied with the idea of leaving it as is and avoiding the irony of spending down earth's resources for a mere flight of fancy.

Build a browser-based pixel art game in the style of a 1980s arcade game — chunky pixels, limited color palette (16-32 colors), no smooth gradients, everything dithered.

Scene: A wide beach. Mostly flat sand. A few beach umbrellas and chairs scattered in the background, sparse. The horizon sits about one third from the top of the screen — ocean beyond it, waves rolling in continuously from the horizon and crashing as foaming whitecaps at the shoreline. Each wave deposits birds on the sand as it retreats. The sky above the horizon is occupied by a fluffy white clouds. The clouds are suspending dark, dense, silhouettes of large data centers visibly embedded in and perching above them. The clouds sit high initially but descend slowly and continuously over time.

Volunteers: Pixel-art human figures walk the beach. Their default behavior is to approach beached birds and paint them with oil — a brief animation, then the bird changes color and begins slowly floating upward toward the cloud layer. Each bird that reaches the clouds visibly lifts them a few pixels. Enough birds and the clouds rise; too few and they descend.

Resource split mechanic: The player can click or tap volunteers to redirect them from manual painting to a data-labeling station at the edge of the screen — a small terminal or clipboard icon. Volunteers at stations contribute to an automation meter. As the meter reaches thresholds, auto-painter drones appear and begin painting birds without volunteer assignment. The tradeoff: divert too many volunteers too fast and unpainted birds pile up faster than the auto-painters can handle; stay fully manual and you can never keep pace as wave frequency increases over time. Waves arrive faster and deposit more birds as the game progresses.

Win state: Clouds fully lifted and held, hiding any data centers just in time to reveal a setting sun as volunteers change into clothes suitable for a Miami-style sunset beach scene. Beach volleyball, sunbathers looking up to enjoy the sunset, drinks, flamingos, dolphins leaping in the sparkling waveless ocean. Still pixel art, but warm golden and pink tones, festive, crowded with life. Hold this state for before the game ends with a celebration screen.

Fail state: Progressive encroachment. First the sky shrinks as clouds descend. Then the ocean horizon disappears behind a low mist. Then the mist reaches the beach itself, turning everything grey and obscured. Then the data centers make contact with the sand and waves — the waves wash over them, and birds riding each wave begin landing on the data center rooftops and painting them white with deposits. The data centers are now the rocks. Game over screen.

Pacing: The game should feel manageable for the first 90 seconds — enough time to understand the mechanic. Wave frequency should increase noticeably around the two-minute mark. Auto-painters should feel meaningful but never sufficient on their own — manual volunteers should always matter. Cloud descent rate should feel slow enough to be hopeful and fast enough to create pressure. The player should always feel like they're one good decision away from stabilizing.

Aesthetic notes: The birds should be simple, recognizable pixel seabirds — maybe a gull silhouette. Painted birds should have a visible oil-sheen color shift, maybe dark iridescent pixels. Floating painted birds should drift slowly with a gentle bobbing motion. Waves should have a looping animation with visible whitecap pixels. No music required but simple chiptune sound effects for wave crash, bird landing, painting completion, and cloud shift would be ideal if achievable.
