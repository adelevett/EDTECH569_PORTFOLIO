Cloudlift Game Concept

Should I use the expanding capabilities of today's AI to generate an arcade-style game critical of AI's environmental impacts? Probably not, which is why instead of a fun game to play you get a poorly drawn sketch and the prompt that most likely would have sufficed to create the game at the "click of a button" (Ropek, 2026).
Ropek, L. (2026, June 9). Anthropic’s Fable 5 can make weirdly fun video games with the click of a button. TechCrunch. https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/09/anthropics-fable-5-can-make-weirdly-fun-video-games-with-the-click-of-a-button/

Cloudlift Game Prompt

Build a browser-based pixel art game in the style of a 1980s arcade game — chunky pixels, limited color palette (16-32 colors), no smooth gradients, everything dithered.

Scene

A wide beach. Mostly flat sand. A few beach umbrellas and chairs scattered in the background, sparse. The horizon sits about one third from the top of the screen — ocean beyond it, waves rolling in continuously from the horizon and crashing as foaming whitecaps at the shoreline. Each wave deposits birds on the sand as it retreats. The sky above the horizon is occupied by a fluffy white clouds. The clouds are suspending dark, dense, silhouettes of large data center equipment visibly embedded in and perching above them resembling a skyline illuminated with activity lights. The clouds sit high initially but descend slowly and continuously over time.

Volunteers

Pixel-art human figures walk the beach. Their default behavior is to approach beached birds and paint them with oil from a paint bucket — a brief animation, then the bird changes color and begins slowly floating upward toward the cloud layer. Each bird that reaches the clouds visibly lifts them a few pixels and animates the activity lights of the data center equipment. Enough birds and the clouds rise; too few and they descend.

Resource Split Mechanic

The player taps and swpies over each bird to trigger a volunteer to perform the action. Players can alternatively redirect volunteers from manual painting to a data-labeling station at the edge of the screen by tapping and dragging them to the small terminal station. Volunteers at stations contribute to an automation meter. As the meter reaches thresholds, auto-painter drones appear from the data center above and begin painting birds without manual click and swipe. Over time, larger drones may also deposit more data labeling stations to accommodate more volunteers. The tradeoff: divert too many volunteers too fast and unpainted birds pile up faster than the terminal can generate auto-painters; stay fully manual and you can never keep pace as wave frequency increases over time, depositing more birds as the game progresses.

Win State

Clouds fully lifted and held, hiding any data centers just in time to reveal a setting sun as volunteers transform into crowds representative of a Miami-style sunset beach scene. Beach volleyball, sunbathers looking up to enjoy the sunset, drinks, flamingos, dolphins leaping in the sparkling waveless ocean. Still pixel art, but warm golden and pink tones, festive, crowded with life. Hold this state for before the game ends with a celebration screen.

Fail State

Progressive encroachment. As more birds crowd the beach, clouds vsibly descend weighed down by lack of activity in the data center. Eventually the ocean horizon disappears behind a low mist and the mist reaches the beach itself, turning everything grey and obscured, eventually making recovery increasingly impossible. Finally the data center makes contact with the sand and slowly sinks as waves wash over them. Birds riding each wave begin landing on the data center and painting the equipment white with deposits. Game over screen.

Pacing

The game should feel manageable for the first 90 seconds — enough time to get oriented and understand the mechanics. Wave frequency should increase noticeably around the two-minute mark. Auto-painters must be slow enough to generate to impact the game only over time, making them never quite sufficient on their own — manual volunteers should always matter. Cloud descent rate should feel slow enough to be hopeful and fast enough to create pressure. The player should always feel like they're one good decision away from stabilizing.

Aesthetic Notes

The birds should be simple, recognizable pixel seabirds — maybe a gull silhouette. Painted birds should have a visible oil-sheen color shift, maybe dark iridescent pixels. Floating painted birds should drift slowly with a gentle bobbing motion. Waves should have a looping animation with visible whitecap pixels. No music required but simple chiptune sound effects for wave crash, bird landing, painting completion, and cloud shift would be ideal if achievable.
